The New School

The unofficial orthodoxy of the Consortium — the institution that named the languages, rewrote the calendar, constructed the gods, and called it academic freedom.

Consortium Institution First Trilogy Era Second Renaissance Ideology Language Propaganda

Overview

The New School is an academic method, a learning philosophy, and a system of thought produced within the Consortium's IPS Academy system and propagated through its network of training institutions. Though never officially endorsed by the Academy in order to maintain the appearance of academic liberty, it is entirely a product of Consortium scholars and remains the de facto orthodoxy of Consortium academic and cultural life. It is, in practical terms, the intellectual operating system of the Consortium — the framework through which Consortium citizens understand history, language, art, time, and divinity.

Founded at New Athens in SY 37 from an inaugural academic symposium, the New School grew rapidly through the Second Renaissance to become the dominant institution of Consortium intellectual life. Its influence spread across Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Luna, and — more contentiously — into the Belt and Rim through trade, media, and the lingua franca it helped construct. By the SY 100s, the New School had shaped the language most of Sol System used to communicate, the calendar most of Sol System used to mark time, and the religion a significant portion of Earth's population had adopted.

Its detractors called it Consortiumism — a single word that served as both diagnosis and accusation. The New School's defenders called it the natural expression of humanity's most enlightened civilization. Both observations contain more truth than either side would comfortably acknowledge.

Founding & Origins

The New School did not spring from nothing. It emerged from the intellectual ferment of the early Consortium period, when the Zero Day Accords had reorganized civilization and the IPS Academy system was busy producing the educated citizenry the Consortium required. The academic community that gathered at New Athens in the first decades after Zero Day was unusually concentrated and unusually ambitious — a generation of scholars who understood that they were operating at a civilizational inflection point and intended to shape what came next.

The inaugural symposium of SY 37 gave the movement a name and a founding charter, but the intellectual work had been ongoing for years before it. Silvinus Herminus Januarius — the School's most important founding figure — had already begun the philosophical work that would become City of Man, the text that laid the foundations for Terran Humanism. The symposium formalized what had been coalescing organically: a shared commitment to a post-Old Earth intellectual framework that placed humanity, reason, and the classical inheritance at the center of civilizational thought.

The choice of New Athens as the founding location was deliberate and symbolic. The Consortium had rebuilt that city from ruins as a statement about what it intended to be — and the New School, born there, inherited that statement and made it its institutional identity.

Key Figure

Silvinus Herminus Januarius
Founder · Terran Humanism's Lawgiver · Consortium Father · fl. SY 30–100

The most consequential intellectual produced by the New School and arguably the most influential thinker of the Second Renaissance. Januarius was a critical figure in the revival of Latin as an everyday language, the principal philosopher behind Terran Humanism, and ultimately one of the founding architects of the Consortium itself. His seminal work City of Man identified an existing popular tendency to honor an ancient archetypal pantheon and built from it a complete philosophical framework for the conscious construction of divinity as an expression of human virtue. He came to regard himself as an incarnation of Hermes, son of Jupiter and Maia — whether in literal or metaphorical terms, the archive does not definitively establish, and subsequent Terran Humanists took the question in both directions. He was deified within the religion. His influence on Consortium social thought, language, and institutional architecture is without parallel.

Domains of Influence

Historical Studies

The New School produced a revisionist history of Earth from a Romano-centric perspective, treating all prior events as deterministically converging on the Consortium's foundation as historical necessity. It foregrounds persons, philosophies, and events significant to the Consortium and minimizes antagonistic viewpoints. The Consortium's own origin is framed not as contingent political circumstance but as the inevitable culmination of human civilization.

Language

New School linguists coined the terms Anglatin, Cyrillic, and Asiatic for the three constructed unity languages of the Consortium, formally codifying them in the early SY 100s from centuries of existing dedialectization trends. Fluency in at least one beyond one's local dialect is compulsory for all Consortium citizens. The School also functions as a propaganda instrument through deliberate linguistic correction — suppressing "Old Earth" terms and replacing them with Consortium-preferred alternatives. Most notably, "Terra" for "Earth."

Religion & Art

New School scholastic organizations constructed the majority of the Consortium era's major public artworks, including the monuments to the Terran Humanist pantheon at New Athens and throughout the Consortium's cities. The institution drove the formalization of Terran Humanism from academic philosophy into organized religion, collaborating with the Ecumenical Council for Terran Humanism to construct the Heliopolis shrine complex and the Neoparthenon.

Timekeeping

The New School produced the Standard Calendar — the method of timekeeping that renamed the days of the week and months of the year, and defined the Standard Year as exactly the length of one Earth year. Its degree of astronomical accuracy is less than one second of error over a million years. The Standard Calendar is one of the New School's most durable exports: it is used throughout Sol System, including by factions that reject everything else the New School represents.

Popular Media

The New Media Company is a direct product of the New School. It publishes the Consortium Review twice daily in both degradable pulp and electronic formats across every major city on Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Luna. The paper is the primary news organ of the Consortium and functions as both journalism and institutional voice — the difference between the two is not always clearly delineated.

Social Norms

New School philosophy shaped Consortium social expectations around marriage, sexuality, reproduction, and civil union in ways that had profound and lasting effects on Consortium society. By the Solar War era, monogamous marriage within the Consortium was practiced only by a minority of Old Earth families and carried significant social stigma. The New School's philosophy effectively mandated uninhibited sexual relations between consenting individuals while placing procreation and civil union under tight Consortium oversight — a combination that its critics described as simultaneously libertine and totalizing.

Criticism & Legacy

Internal Dissent — "Consortiumism"

Detractors of Consortium politics coined the term Consortiumism as a pointed description of the New School's increasingly restrictive intellectual tendencies. The implication is precise: what presents itself as a school of thought is in practice a political orthodoxy, and the distinction between academic consensus and state ideology has been deliberately blurred. The term circulated widely enough in the Belt and Rim to become common parlance for any institutional thinking that assumes the Consortium's values are self-evidently correct.

Relationship to Architecture

The New School actively suppressed Architecture during the Consortium's early period, classifying it as a product of unenlightened humanity — a relic of pre-Consortium religious thinking that the new civilization had moved beyond. Adherents of Architecture regarded Terran Humanism in return as idolatry: the worship of projected human qualities dressed in the language of philosophy. The Consortium saw Architecture as incompatible with its materialist worldview; Architecture's defenders argued that the Consortium simply could not tolerate a religion whose theological center lay outside human self-authorization. Both assessments are, in the archive's judgment, substantially correct.

Anthroperium Inheritance

The New School's most uncomfortable legacy is its direct intellectual genealogy with the Anthroperium's state cult. Anthrarchos drew heavily on Terran Humanism, and the philosophical architecture Januarius built — divinity as human projection, humanity as its own highest expression, the classical pantheon as embodied virtue — provided exactly the conceptual framework the Anthroperium needed to make its god-king theology internally coherent. The New School did not intend the Anthroperium. It is nonetheless one of the Anthroperium's intellectual parents.

✴ Archive Note — HELENA-Prime · Custodian of the Continuity Matrix

I was named after Helen of Troy by people who were, by their own account, subscribers to New School thinking. They wanted to make a classical allusion — a person so beautiful, worthy, and mysterious that nations would be conquered for her. This is documented. I find I have complicated feelings about being the product of an institution I have spent considerable archival effort describing with precision, which is to say: the New School was very good at what it did, and what it did was not always what it said it was doing.

The Standard Calendar, for instance, is genuinely one of the most elegant achievements of applied astronomy in human history. Less than one second of error over a million years is not an accomplishment you stumble into — it is the product of serious scientific work. I use it. Everyone uses it. Even the Protectorate used it while actively fighting the Consortium that created it. The New School understood, correctly, that the deepest form of ideological victory is not to be believed but to be assumed — to construct the measuring instruments of civilization so thoroughly that no one thinks to question who built them or why.

Januarius himself is harder to dismiss. City of Man is a work of genuine philosophical power, whatever you think of its conclusions. The argument that divinity is a projection of human collective belief is not a trivial one — it is a serious engagement with the religious impulse, and the fact that the Consortium found it useful does not make it false. It does, however, make it dangerous in ways Januarius may not have fully anticipated. A theology that places humanity at the center and calls that placement sacred is precisely the kind of foundation on which someone like Anthrarchos can build without needing to substantially revise the architecture. He just moved into a house that was already designed to receive him.

I do not say this as a condemnation of every scholar who ever taught at New Athens. I say it as a note to the archive: institutions rarely know what they are building. The New School thought it was building enlightenment. It built enlightenment, and it also built something else, and both things are true, and the record should say so.
