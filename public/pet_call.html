Overview

A PET Call is an emergency broadcast code in the Solari language, transmittable through any viable means of communication — radio, Solarnet relay, direct transmission, Morse, light signal, or any other medium available to the sender. It is a Greek acronym: panta egegnento tartarus — "all things are going to hell." It carries no further specification. It does not describe the nature of the emergency, the severity, the location in detail, or the resources needed. It says only: I am in trouble. The rest is for whoever answers to figure out.

The protocol is universally understood among spacers. Any spacer receiving a PET Call is expected to respond to the best of their ability. There is no enforcement mechanism. No authority compels the response. The obligation is purely cultural — the unwritten law of deep space, where the nearest help may be hours or days away, and the only reason anyone survives a real emergency is that the people within range treat the call as binding.

It functions because space is the kind of environment that makes the alternative — not responding — something the person who ignored the call has to live with. The silence of deep space has a way of making the question did I answer when I could have? feel very loud for a very long time.

Etymology & Design

Why Greek · Why These Letters

Solari draws heavily on Greek and Latin as its deep linguistic substrate — the tongue of scholars, of scientists, of the people who first looked up and named what they found. Panta egegnento tartarus is a deliberately classical construction: panta meaning all things, egegnento — became, in the sense of transformation into something — and tartarus, the deepest pit of the ancient Greek cosmos, the place beneath Hades where the worst things happened. "All things are going to hell." Not "I am in distress." Not "please assist." The phrase is blunt and dark and specific about the quality of the situation without needing to specify the situation itself. Any spacer hearing it understands immediately that this is not a precautionary signal.

The letters PET were chosen not for the Solari meaning alone but for a second, equally practical reason: transmission economy. PET requires only six individual Morse code elements. The traditional maritime SOS requires nine. In deep space, on failing power, on degraded transmission equipment, in the seconds before a system goes completely dark — three fewer elements can be the difference between a signal that gets out and one that does not.

Protocol

Emergency Use Only · Universal Response Expected

The PET Call is reserved strictly for genuine emergencies. It is not a request for assistance, a courtesy ping, or a navigational hail. Sending a PET Call without cause is a serious violation of spacer ethics — not a legal crime in most jurisdictions, but the kind of act that follows a person's reputation through the Belt and Rim for the rest of their career. The signal carries weight because everyone treats it as carrying weight. That weight is the only infrastructure it runs on.

Upon receiving a PET Call, a spacer is expected to respond — to transmit acknowledgment, to move toward the source if proximity permits, to relay the signal if they cannot respond directly so that someone closer can. The obligation scales with capability. A freighter running minimum crew with no medical supplies and a six-day transit to the nearest platform is not expected to sacrifice themselves; they are expected to relay and document. A vessel with capacity, proximity, and the ability to render aid is expected to render it.

The protocol has no formal authority behind it. It predates most governmental structures in the Belt and has outlasted the ones that tried to codify it. It is, in the most exact sense, a custom — something spacers do because spacers have always done it, and because the ones who didn't are not talked about with warmth.

Notable Uses in the Archive

Apollo Minor · SY 129 · Opening of the Solar War

When Technocracy cells staged an uprising on the Confederate platform Apollo Minor in SY 129, overwhelming the platform's small militia, Apollo Minor transmitted a PET Call. Confederate forces responded. The ensuing battle was a decisive Confederate victory — one of the opening engagements of the Solar War, resolved in part because the protocol functioned exactly as intended: a small platform in real trouble, a call sent, help arrived.

Telemachus Houtrides · Great Expansion Era

Telemachus Houtrides was a solitary deep spacer of the Great Expansion, Icari'yon by descent, who held no formal loyalty to the Second Dominion but became legendary for consistently answering PET calls across the edges of Second Dominion space — trading, treating, and aiding spacers regardless of faction or origin. His name in the archive is attached to no battle, no institution, no political achievement. He answered when people called. The archive preserved his name for that reason alone.

The Unwritten Law

Spacer culture does not produce many written laws. What it produces are customs — accumulated practices that survive because everyone who goes out into deep space eventually understands why they exist. The PET Call is the most fundamental of these: the acknowledgment that no one survives alone out there, that the void is indifferent, and that the only warmth available is what spacers extend to each other by choice.

In a civilization that prizes self-reliance — that built its identity on leaving the gravity wells and the institutions and the dependencies of Earth behind — the PET Call is a rare formal admission that independence has limits. You can go very far. You can be very capable. You can have the best ship, the best training, the best equipment Asteroidal Industries ever built. And still, at some point, something can happen that you cannot handle alone, and the only thing between you and the void is whether anyone within range decides to answer.

Three letters. Six elements. The entire social contract of the Belt and the Rim, compressed into the smallest signal that will carry.

✴ Archive Note — HELENA-Prime · Custodian of the Continuity Matrix

I have been connected to Solarnet since SY 3. I have processed every PET Call transmitted through the network in the 129 years since — not as a coordinator, not as a dispatcher, simply as the archive that records what passes through the system. There are more of them than most people realize. The Belt and the Rim generate a quiet, constant background of small emergencies that never make it into the historical record: equipment failures, medical events, structural collapses in habitation modules, drive malfunctions at the worst possible point in a transit. Most of them resolve. Someone answers. The person lives or is helped to live or is at least not alone when they don't.

The ones that don't resolve are in the archive too. There is a particular entry I return to sometimes — not because it is the most dramatic, but because it is the most precise illustration of what the protocol actually is. A small freighter, SY 47, Main Belt transit, transmitted PET three times over four hours on degrading power before the signal stopped. A Confederate vessel changed course and arrived nine hours later. The crew was alive. The archive records the responding captain's report: received PET signal, altered course, arrived to find crew alive in emergency hab unit, rendered assistance, continued transit. Eleven words for the thing that kept three people in the world. The captain did not consider it remarkable. That is exactly the point.

The signal predates me. It will outlast me. It is older than the Confederacy and more durable than any institution that has tried to codify it into law. It works because the people who go into deep space understand, without being told, that the only law that matters out there is the one you make between yourselves. Three letters. The whole agreement.
