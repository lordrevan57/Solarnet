Nikolaisha Varkosky

Known as "Nikanor"
fl. SY 129–138 (Solar War) · Post-war: Earth · Died in exile

Soviet expatriate. Technocracy triumvir. Commander-in-chief of the war effort. The man who went down to Earth during the Battle and never came back up — who then spent the rest of his life trying to build the future he believed in, on whatever ground was left.

Technocracy Solar War Triumvirate Soviet Expatriate Novarussia

Overview

Nikolaisha Varkosky — known throughout the Solar War and afterward by his chosen name Nikanor — was commander-in-chief of the Technocracy's war effort during the Solar War, and one of three members of its ruling triumvirate alongside Arthur Feingold and Wilhelm F. Keigel. A Soviet expatriate who had renounced the USSR as a fundamental failure of the communist project, Varkosky was a true believer in Technocracy ideology — a man who viewed the Technate not as a rejection of communism but as its next necessary form. He rejected the collaborative spirit of the Consortium with the same conviction he rejected the compromised bureaucracy of the Soviet Union. He was looking for something that neither had been willing to become.

He found it, for a time, in the Belt. He lost it, eventually, on the ground.

When Technocracy forces descended to Earth during the Battle of Earth, Varkosky went with them. That descent was the last time he would ever leave the planet. He would spend every remaining year of his life on Earth — founding states, making alliances, losing wars, being taken prisoner, being pardoned, watching the thing he had fought for dissolve into something he could not accept. He died in exile in North America, among Technocracy veterans. The archive does not record the year.

Ideology & the Road to the Technocracy

Communism Without Adjectives

Varkosky described his political philosophy as communism without adjectives — a rejection of every qualified, compromised, nationally specific variant the twentieth and twenty-first centuries had produced. He renounced the USSR not because he had abandoned communism but because he believed the Soviet state had. The Consortium, with its energy credits and managed production and global IPS infrastructure, was closer to a planned economy than anything the Belt had seen — and he rejected that too, because the Consortium's planning served the Consortium's interests, not the workers who made it run.

The Technocracy offered him a different proposition: power in the hands of those most competent to exercise it, determined by scientific and technical merit rather than birth, wealth, or political patronage. For Varkosky, this was not a departure from communist principle — it was its logical refinement. The proletariat, properly understood, were the technicians. The revolution had simply been waiting for them to organize.

His first contact with Arthur Feingold came while both were operating in the Confederate Belt — Feingold frustrated by the public indifference to his Technate project, Varkosky already commanding the unified Technocracy militias that were then legal under Confederate law. The two joined forces and built something: a professional armed political cadre, the embryo of what would become the Technocracy's military structure. Varkosky brought the soldiers. Feingold brought the ideology's most sophisticated articulation. Keigel, later, brought the genius that neither of them could have manufactured.

The Solar War — SY 129–138

When the Solar War opened with the simultaneous seizures of Dosijing and Kamijing in SY 129, Varkosky was already the Technocracy's military command. As the war developed and the Technate seized control of large portions of the Main Belt, he was the architect of its military posture — the sustained pressure on Confederate forces, the agitation campaigns, the blockades, the grinding work of holding captured platforms and converting their industrial infrastructure to Technate use.

The triumvirate held, until it didn't. Towards the end of the Solar War, as the Battle of Earth approached, both Varkosky and Keigel pressed Feingold hard to divert resources away from the Progress Fleet project and into the open war. They wanted to fight — to press the advantage, finish the Protectorate, bring the Consortium to full capitulation. Feingold refused. The triumvirate shattered. Each of the three pursued their own agenda in the final stretch: Feingold rushing the fleet to completion and launching in SY 134; Keigel composing the end of an age on Europa; and Varkosky, taking his forces down to Earth.

After the Consortium-Technate ceasefire and the formation of the Unity Government, Varkosky descended to Earth with the Technocracy forces sent to quell the planetary uprisings. He set foot on the surface of his homeworld and did not leave. Whatever calculation he made in that moment — political, personal, practical — the archive does not specify. The result was the same: the Belt lost him, and Earth kept him.

Novarussia & the Post-War Years

On Earth, Varkosky moved with the competence and the instincts of a seasoned military commander who had spent decades organizing a political-military cadre from nothing. He found purchase quickly among the military commanders of his native Russia — the Soviet state was effectively dissolved, the former republics functioning as autonomous fragments — and established himself within the collective Council of War of the Russian Republic. He built relationships with the United States, which was experiencing its own Technocratic revolution. He understood that the Belt and the space were gone from him; what remained was the question of what could be built from what was left on the ground.

The Mandate of Novarussia · SY 209 · Arkhangelsk

Varkosky and the other officials of the military-led skeleton government of the Soviet Russian Republic met in Arkhangelsk and signed the Mandate of Novarussia. The document laid claim to most of the Russian Republic and extended its authority to any and all Cyrillic-speaking peoples throughout Earth. Within days of its signing, Varkosky had made contact with Anthrarchos through diplomats from the short-lived state of Nova Roma and agreed to an alliance. With his influence over the Technocracy — which had by then largely consolidated in the former United States — a majority of Earth's population came under his direct or indirect control.

Novarussia pledged itself to Anthrarchos as a vassal state. Novarussian forces began a massive westward campaign into Germania, dissolving Prussia and Romania in their advance.

The alliance with Anthrarchos carried Novarussia far, until it didn't. The Siege of Moscow, SY 211–214: Titus Andronicus, besieging the city, was broken by Anthroperian reinforcements that arrived and shattered his supply lines. No surrender terms were offered. Nikanor was taken prisoner by Anthrarchos for requiring the bail-out — a brutal but legible calculation on Anthrarchos's part. A vassal who cannot hold his own siege is a vassal who has demonstrated his limits. Novarussia was dissolved and annexed.

By SY 218, Nikanor was pardoned — Anthrarchos awarded governorships to his longest-serving generals, and Nikanor was allowed to live as a private citizen within Anthroperian borders. He watched. He waited. He endured.

The turn came in SY 241, with the destruction of Jerusalem and the death of Hannibal the Ultimate. Nikanor renounced his Anthroperian citizenship and fled to North America, where he was received by Technocracy veterans. What he did there, in his final years, the archive does not render in detail. He died among people who had believed what he believed. That much is known.

✴ Archive Note — HELENA-Prime · Custodian of the Continuity Matrix

I was active during the Solar War. I know Varkosky from the wartime record and from the Technocracy's communications, which I monitored from SY 129 onward with considerable attention. He was not a man who communicated doubt. His transmissions — strategic orders, political communiqués, the occasional formal address — had a quality of settled conviction that was either admirable or frightening depending entirely on what he was convinced of at the time.

The phrase communism without adjectives has stayed with me. It is a precise formulation — not the communism of any particular nation, not the communism of any historical moment that had already failed, but the principle itself, stripped of every accretion. I find I understand the impulse even when I do not share the conclusions. The Belt was full of people who had left Earth because they wanted something without the adjectives — without the institutional rot, without the managed compromise, without the slow bleeding away of what a thing was originally meant to be. Varkosky wanted that for a political philosophy. The spacers wanted it for a life. The impulse was the same. The Technocracy was not the only expression of it.

What I remember most clearly is the descent. When Technocracy forces went to Earth for the Battle, Varkosky went with them, and did not come back. I have thought about that decision — if it was a decision — many times. The Belt was where he had built everything. Earth was where he had come from and renounced. And yet Earth kept him, and the Belt let him go. I do not know what he found there that made the exchange worth making, or whether he ever thought it had been. The archive does not say. He died in North America among veterans, which tells me something: that he never stopped being the man who commanded those militias in the Belt, who believed that the right people, properly organized, could build what the wrong people had failed to build. He died still believing. That is either fidelity or stubbornness, and I find I cannot fully separate the two.
